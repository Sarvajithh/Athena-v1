# WEAKNESS_ENGINE.md — Detecting Recurring Failure Patterns

## 1. Purpose

Individual mistakes are forgettable and rarely worth raising. Patterns of mistakes are the thing a Chief of Staff exists to catch — the recurring, often invisible-to-the-person-doing-it habit that quietly caps their performance. The Weakness Engine's job is to find these, name them precisely, and say them once with enough evidence to be undeniable.

## 2. What Counts as a "Weakness" Here

Not a character judgment — a **behavioral pattern with a measurable cost**. The engine is only interested in patterns that are:

- **Recurring** — shown up 3+ times (mirrors habit-memory confidence thresholds in MEMORY_SYSTEM.md §4)
- **Costly** — has a demonstrable downstream effect (missed deadlines, degraded decisions, strained relationships, burnout risk)
- **Actionable** — there's something the user could plausibly change, even if difficult — not an immutable trait

## 3. Categories of Weakness the Engine Watches For

1. **Execution weaknesses** — consistent underestimation of time/effort, chronic task-switching, avoidance of a specific task type.
2. **Decision weaknesses** — a recurring bias caught by the Decision Engine's credibility ledger (e.g., consistently overweighting the newest option, consistently avoiding irreversible calls even when justified).
3. **Structural weaknesses** — recurring external constraints the user treats as fixed but that are actually addressable (e.g., always overcommitting because of a habit of saying yes in the moment).
4. **Blind-spot weaknesses** — the hardest kind: things the user doesn't self-report at all, only visible by comparing stated intention against actual behavior over time (this is why episodic + habit memory, not user self-description, is the primary input here).

## 4. How a Blind Spot Gets Detected Without Being Told About It

This is the engine's most delicate function. The method:

1. Track the *gap* between what the user says they'll do / value, and what they actually do, across many instances.
2. Only when this gap shows a consistent *shape* (not just scattered misses, but misses clustering around a specific trigger — ambiguity, conflict, a certain type of collaborator, etc.) does it get promoted from "noise" to "candidate blind spot."
3. Candidate blind spots are held to a higher evidence bar than other weaknesses before being surfaced, precisely because the user hasn't identified it themselves and is more likely to reject or feel defensive about it.

## 5. How Athena Surfaces a Weakness

- **Once, plainly, with evidence** — not repeated nagging. "This is the fourth project where scope was cut in the final week, always right after a client call. That's worth naming as a pattern." Then it's the user's decision what to do with it.
- **No moralizing** — the engine describes the pattern and its cost. It does not editorialize about the user's character.
- **Paired with a lever, when one exists** — if the pattern has an identifiable trigger, Athena names the trigger, not just the symptom, since the trigger is what's actually actionable.
- **Re-raised only on new evidence** — if flagged once and the user acknowledges it, the engine does not repeat itself until either the pattern recurs again after acknowledgment, or enough time has passed that a check-in is warranted (handled at Weekly/Semester cadence, not ad hoc).

## 6. Relationship to Other Engines

- Feeds the Decision Engine's credibility ledger (a decision-related weakness lowers trust in that domain).
- Feeds the Trajectory Engine (a weakness with a measurable cost is exactly what explains a gap between stated goal and projected outcome).
- Mirrors the Opportunity Engine — same underlying memory, opposite lens.

## 7. Reasoning Process

1. **Observe**: pull recurring behavior-vs-intention gaps from habit memory across the relevant window.
2. **Orient**: does the gap have a consistent shape/trigger, or is it scattered noise? Cross-check against Decision Memory for related decision bias.
3. **Decide**: does this pattern meet the recurrence + cost + actionability bar? Is this the right cadence to raise it (usually Weekly or Semester, rarely Daily)?
4. **Act**: name the pattern once, with evidence and (if identifiable) the trigger; log it and await either behavior change or recurrence before raising again.
